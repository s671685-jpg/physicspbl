Background Knowledge

🌊 What is a Wave? — Quick Recap

A wave is a disturbance that transfers energy from one place to another without moving matter.

Light is an electromagnetic wave — it doesn't need anything to travel through!

λ
Wavelength
Distance between wave peaks
f
Frequency
Waves per second (Hz)
A
Amplitude
Height of the wave
v
Speed
How fast it travels

Wave diagram

v = f × λ

speed = frequency × wavelength

Evidence #1

🔀 What is Diffraction?

Diffraction = when waves bend around obstacles or spread out through narrow openings

What happens:

  • When light passes through a narrow slit, it doesn't just go straight — it SPREADS OUT
  • The smaller the slit (vs wavelength), the MORE spreading occurs
  • This spreading is called diffraction and ONLY waves can do this
  • Particles would just go straight through — no spreading!
  • This is strong evidence that light behaves as a wave 🌊
Slide 6 / 13
Evidence #2

🤝 Constructive vs Destructive Interference

When two waves meet, they interact — this is called INTERFERENCE

✅ CONSTRUCTIVE
Two crests meet OR two troughs meet
The waves ADD TOGETHER and get BIGGER
Crest + Crest = Double crest! 🌟
= BRIGHT SPOT
VS
❌ DESTRUCTIVE
A crest meets a trough
The waves CANCEL OUT — get smaller or disappear
Crest + Trough = ZERO 🕳️
= DARK SPOT
Slide 7 / 13
Classic Experiment

🔬 Young's Double-Slit Experiment

The most famous experiment proving light is a wave! (Thomas Young, 1801)

1

Shine a laser at a barrier with TWO tiny slits

2

Light passes through both slits at the same time

3

The two waves from each slit spread out (diffract)

4

They meet and interfere with each other

5

On a screen: alternating bright and dark bands

6

These bands (fringes) prove wave behavior! 🎉

Slide 8 / 13
Equations

📐 The Math Behind It

Double-Slit Formula
d · sin(θ) = m · λ
d
d = distance between the two slits
θ
θ = angle to the bright or dark fringe
m
m = order number (0, ±1, ±2...) — which fringe?
λ
λ = wavelength of the light being used

Bright fringes: m = 0, ±1, ±2 …   |   Dark fringes: m = ±½, ±3/2 …

Slide 9 / 13
Applications

🌍 Real Life Examples

These aren't just textbook things — we see them in everyday life!

💿
CDs & DVDs
The rainbow colors on CDs are caused by light diffracting off tiny grooves on the disk surface
🫧
Soap Bubbles
Rainbow colors from thin-film interference — light waves reflecting from both surfaces cancel or add up
🦋
Butterfly Wings
Microscopic wing structures cause diffraction of light, making them shimmer with color!
📡
Radio Waves
Radio waves diffract around buildings — that's why you get signal indoors even without line-of-sight
👁️
Eye Floaters
Blurry dots you see in bright light happen because of diffraction of light inside your eye
🌊
Ocean Waves
Ocean waves diffract around islands and through harbor gaps — exact same principle as light!

Personal Reflection

🤔 My Reflection

Most interesting thing I learned

Before this project, I always thought light was just... light. But finding out that it actually BENDS and creates patterns blew my mind! The soap bubble colors finally make sense now.

Biggest challenge

Understanding destructive interference was confusing at first. How can two waves together equal nothing? But once I drew it out, it clicked — the crest of one fills the trough of the other perfectly.

What surprised me most

Young's experiment was done in 1801! Scientists figured this out over 200 years ago with just light, a barrier, and a screen. That's actually amazing.

How I'd improve my project

I would've liked to try an actual diffraction grating experiment to see the patterns myself. Maybe next time I can use a laser pointer and a CD!

Final Answer

🎯 Conclusion

"Diffraction and interference are BOTH wave behaviors. Since light shows BOTH of them, light MUST behave like a wave. The patterns of bright and dark fringes are impossible to explain any other way."
✅

Diffraction proves light bends around obstacles — a wave property that particles don't have

✅

Constructive interference = bright fringes (waves add up to double the amplitude)

✅

Destructive interference = dark fringes (waves cancel each other out completely)

✅

Young's experiment in 1801 gave measurable, repeatable evidence using the double-slit setup

✅

Real-world examples like CDs and soap bubbles confirm this every single day!
